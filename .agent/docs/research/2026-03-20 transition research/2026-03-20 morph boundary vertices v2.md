
Territory transition is failing because Geometry is still being emitted primarily as draw-ready polygons instead of as a canonical **boundary representation**. That is the root defect. Transition should not be solving a global polygon-correspondence problem after the fact. Geometry must define the frontier in a form that already encodes identity, adjacency, and ownership, so Transition becomes a local boundary-edit problem rather than a shape-matching problem. Boundary representations exist precisely to separate topology from geometry and to make traversal, adjacency, and common-boundary editing well-defined.

The decisive control points are the meaningful frontier vertices: **3-way junctions** and **frontier–map-edge junctions**. These are the anchors. They are not ephemeral render samples; they are structural vertices of the territory topology. Treat them as first-class, identity-bearing geometry. Everything else along the frontier is subordinate to them. Shared-boundary editing systems work by preserving the common edge and its topological anchors, then modifying only the affected segment.

Generate each territory frontier as an ordered loop of canonical boundary vertices and canonical boundary edges. Each vertex must know who and what it is: its stable identity, its coordinates, and its neighboring boundary entities. Each edge must know what lies on its left and right, whether it is owner-owner or owner-world, and exactly how to render from one canonical vertex to the next. That geometric definition may be linear or smoothed, but it must be canonical, not presentation-only. Half-edge or equivalent boundary-topology structures are appropriate because they make loop traversal and shared-boundary identity explicit.

Do **not** assign semantic importance to every sampled render vertex. That is the trap. Sampled points created for smoothing, flattening, or draw resolution are transient. They should be regenerated from the canonical edges whenever needed. Stable identity belongs to the canonical vertices and edges, not to arbitrary resampling artifacts. If you attach identity to sampling output, you are binding Transition to the accidents of discretization instead of to the true frontier structure.

The transition pipeline then becomes straightforward. Build `TMAP-A` from the old ownership state. Apply the conquest event. Build `TMAP-B` from the new ownership state using the exact same geometry rules. Diff the two maps at the level of canonical boundary entities. Most canonical vertices and edges should survive unchanged. The ones that do not survive define a local frontier edit. The divergence point and rejoin point of that edit are the anchor vertices. Everything outside those anchors is stationary by definition and must be copied exactly through the animation.

Animate only the changed boundary patch between the anchors. Extract the changed arc from `TMAP-A` and the changed arc from `TMAP-B`, including whatever smoothing or adjustment Geometry has already defined. Sample those two arcs to equal counts along arc length, preserving order from anchor to anchor, and interpolate sample $i$ in A to sample $i$ in B. Do not infer global correspondence across the whole ring. Do not classify all ring vertices as static or dynamic. Do not rotate or reparameterize the full frontier. The unchanged boundary does not participate in the solve; it is copied verbatim.

This is the correct abstraction: Geometry owns topology, identity, and curve definition; Transition owns diffing canonical boundary edits and interpolating only the changed patch; Presentation owns drawing the exact same per-frame geometry for both fill and border. Fill and stroke must never diverge because neither one is allowed to invent or smooth geometry independently. They both consume the same animated frontier output every frame.

So the imperative is simple. Stop treating territory transition as polygon morphing. Stop using whole-ring resampling as the primary correspondence model. Stop giving semantic status to transient sampled vertices. Build a canonical frontier map with identity-bearing junction vertices and edges. Diff that map across ownership states. Find the two anchors that bound the local edit. Copy the unchanged frontier exactly. Interpolate only the changed arc. That is the clean model, and it is the model the system should be built around.
